Resultative constructions

Georgian resultatives are analytical constructions of the participle + auxiliary verb type:

 cigni dacerilia. / cigni dacerili aris.
 es Pilmi nanaHi maKvs.
 mePe mqavs nanaHi.

However, they are different from superficially similar constructions like the Norwegian perfect, which is analysed as a biclausal control (XCOMP) construction:

 Boken er skrevet.
 Jeg har sett denne filmen/kongen. 

This can be seen when we take a verb with a 1st or 2nd person direct object:

 Sens megobars nanaHi vhqavar.
 Your friends have seen me.

The auxiliary verb `vhqavar' has a 1st person object circumfix `v-...-var', thus, the object cannot be the participle (although, in 3rd person, it matches the object case).
Second reason: `have' is suppletive according to animateness of object.

Why no analysis with XCOMP as extra argument? -> This would result in double control.

So, a complex predicate analysis is appropriate: the auxiliary contributes tense information and tense/number morphology, whereas the the past participle contributes the subcategorization frame and the predicate.

Normally, intransitives and passives use a finite form of `qoPna' "to be" as auxiliary, whereas transitives go with `Kona' or `qola', depending on the animacy of the direct object.

Here, the case alignment patterns like in the perfect series and matches the case alignment of `Kona'/`qola' "to have", if we assume that they are transitive predicates:

There are, however, resultatives with `Kona'/`qola' "to have" where the dative argument is not the agentive subject. To understand these constructions better, we have to look at the verbs `qoPna' "to be" and `Kona'/`qola' "to have" in more detail.

In Georgian, as we already saw, `Kona'/`qola' "to have" is a class IV or indirect verb. The possessor is in the dative, where the possessee is the nominative argument of the verb. I assume that the possessor is the subject of the predicate.
But, in addition to express the concept of possession, the verbs `Kona'/`qola' also function as applicative versions of the predicative `qoPna' "to be", as is apparent from these and similar examples:
